The Geography of the Middle East

Objectives

Students will: 

  • Consider the diverse nature of countries, peoples, and geographies of the Middle East.
  • Practice general map-reading skills.
  • Locate the Middle East, its countries, significant cities, and geographical features on a map.
  • Work together to explore photos of countries in the region and identify issues and themes for inquiry.

Required Reading

This lesson can be done prior to beginning the reading as to introduce students to the importance of thinking about the Middle East as a diverse group of countries and peoples. It can also be done after students have read the Introduction and Part I and completed “Study Guide—Introduction and Part I”  or “Advanced Study Guide—Introduction and Part I.” 

Note

  • Colored pencils might be helpful as students fill in their maps.
  • Teachers may want students to refer to their maps as they continue reading.

Resources

In the Classroom

  1. Focus Question—Pose the question “What is a generalization?” Then ask students “What is North America?” Ask students to name the countries in North America. Do students think the following generalizations about North America are true?

    • People in North America speak English.
    • The people of North America value economic wealth more than anything else.
    • People in North America are extremely religious.
    • The climate in North America is usually very harsh, with hot, humid summers and cold, snowy winters.
    • The people of North America have different values than people from other parts of the world.
    • Women in North America often drop out of college to have children.

    Review some of the objections that students raised to these generalizations. Emphasize the difficulty of generalizing about a whole region. Highlight the idea that while there are sometimes shared characteristics, a region is made up of different countries and diverse peoples. Why do students think that people make generalizations about a group of people or a region? How would students feel if they heard someone making generalizations about their region, country, state, town, school, religion, or culture? Can some generalizations be valid or useful? How can students avoid making generalizations that are not valid or useful?

    Remind students that they are going to begin studying the Middle East. How might they apply their thoughts about generalizations to learning about the Middle East? Why might this be important?
  2. a. Exploring the Geography of the Middle East—Divide the class into groups of three or four. Distribute the worksheet and maps to each group. Students should read the instructions carefully before beginning the exercise.

    b. Photo Analysis—Assign each group one of the sets of photographs. Each of the sets of photographs is tied to a case study they will read in Part IV of the reading. Instruct students to view the photos, briefly summarize what they see, mark the general location of their photo collection on their “Middle East” map, and work together to develop a question about their photos to share with their classmates.

    Note: Point out that it is important to be careful about drawing conclusions from photos, and remind students that they cannot be certain that a photo is an accurate or complete reflection of reality. While photos can provide clues about societies and how people live, students should be aware that photos, like written documents, show only a small piece of a bigger picture. It might be helpful for teachers to use one of the photos to model the difference between observation and inference. Teachers should highlight that inferences can be incorrect or different from what others infer about the same photo. For an interesting view of the issue of making assumptions about appearances, you might show Professor Jenny White’s video “How does veiling differ across countries?”
  3. Sharing Conclusions—Call on students to share their findings. Did any of the photos change students’ ideas or assumptions? How did these photos challenge generalizations students have heard about the Middle East? What are the benefits of using photographs as a source for learning about other countries and societies? What are the limitations of using photographs as a source for learning about the countries and peoples of the Middle East? How might photos present a selective or misleading portrait of a place or society? For example, how difficult might it be for students to pick four or five photos that fully and accurately depict their home country?

    Have the photographs raised any new questions about life and societies in the Middle East? What question did students develop about their collection of photos? Where do students think they might find answers to these questions? Record each group’s question, and tell students to do the same. Challenge students to use their work in the upcoming days to answer these questions. (You could choose to revisit these questions after completing the unit.)

Homework

Students should read Part II and complete “Study Guide—Part II”  or “Advanced Study Guide—Part II.” 

Extra Challenge

Have students collect their own images from any of the countries on the Middle East map. Have them present their photos to the class along with a question raised by each photo.
