Precolonial Poetry of the Middle East

Objectives

Students will: 

  • Read and analyze poetry written by women in pre-Islamic and early Islamic societies.
  • Consider the benefits and limitations of poetry as a source for historical learning.

Required Reading

Students should have read Part I and completed “Study Guide—Introduction and Part I” or “Advanced Study Guide—Introduction and Part I.” 

Resources

  • "Precolonial Poetry" 
  • “Poetry Organizer and Questions” 

Note: Be sure to preview the sources in this lesson to be sure they are appropriate for your classroom. The first poem addresses sexual violence. The second poem includes vocabulary that may be challenging for some students.

In the Classroom

  1. Focus Question—Pose the following questions to students: “What can poetry reveal about history and society? Are there limitations to using poetry to learn about history and society?” Invite student responses. Once students have discussed these questions, tell them that one benefit to studying poetry in the context of the early history of the Middle East is that it can provide insight into the lives, concerns, and perspectives of people of a variety of backgrounds. In this lesson, students will read and analyze poetry written by women in the precolonial Middle East.
  2. Group Analysis—Form groups of two to three students. Distribute “Precolonial Poetry” and “Poetry Organizer and Questions” to all students. Tell students that they are going to read poems or excerpts of poems that highlight a variety of themes and issues. Each group should read all of the poems, but assign each group just two poems to analyze according to the instructions on the handout “Poetry Organizer and Questions.”
  3. Sharing Findings—Reconvene the class. Invite students to share anything that they found especially interesting. Next, ask a representative from each group to present their findings about the poems they analyzed. Who wrote it? When did she live? What did students read about her background? What topics or themes did the poem address? What was the poem’s primary message? Did the poem reveal anything new to students? Do students have any unanswered questions about the poem? Can students think of factors that might have influenced each poet? Who might the intended audience of each poem be? As each group shares, students in the other groups should complete the remaining sections of their graphic organizers, filling in the information for the poems that they did not analyze.
  4. Drawing Conclusions—Once each group has presented, ask students to think about what they learned from the poems overall. Can students think of other sources from which they could have learned the same information? If so, what sources might they have consulted? If not, why might that be the case? (You may wish to remind students that sources produced by marginalized groups—for example women, religious minorities, racial minorities, poor people, and members of the LGBTQ community—have, throughout history, been ignored, destroyed, or silenced in some other way.) Did the poetry raise any new questions for students about the Middle Eastern societies in which they were written? How might students go about answering these questions?

Homework

Students should read Part II and complete “Study Guide—Part II” and “Advanced Study Guide—Part II.”
